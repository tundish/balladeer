Tell me a Story

Story-telling is at the heart of human culture. We all have the right to tell our stories.

In an internet-enabled world, with an open and semantic web, you might think that poetry and literature would proliferate. I have to say I don't see that myself. There are places to find new writing online, but not many. And where they exist, they are not always operated by the authors themselves.

Is it possible for one individual to tell a story to the world, outside of corporate social media? Yes, that is possible. And it has been possible for a number of years. But there is now a free and modern option for those who want to do that.

It is unique in its web-native architecture; it is designed to serve on the Web. It is written in the world's most popular programming language. That's Python, the one they teach in schools.

Its name is Balladeer.

Drama and Dialogue

As an author in a Balladeer project, you supply two types of content; Drama and Dialogue. Drama is code written in Python. It contains all the functionality of your piece. Dialogue goes into text files, which have a structure and syntax akin to that of a traditional screenplay.

If you are writing poetry, there may be no code at all. If your art is a game, there could be many Drama modules for you to develop, along with unit tests to check they work correctly.

Where Next?

The Balladeer blog hosts regular articles on how to use the framework. There is a demo game you can download and run too; it's called Tea and Sympathy.

Balladeer relies heavily on other libraries, known as the Turberfield packages. Each of those has its own online documentation. You may also wish to read about reStructuredText.

Don't forget you can post comments under the blog articles, or raise issues at the code repository.

Have fun telling your Story!
